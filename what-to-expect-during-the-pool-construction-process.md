## **Step 1: Design, Engineering, and Permitting (4–8 Weeks)**

The construction timeline does not begin the day equipment arrives—it begins
the day your permit is submitted. In Palm Beach County, all inground pools
require review by the building, plumbing, and electrical departments. A
licensed structural engineer must stamp the plans before the county will
accept them. This phase typically runs 4 to 8 weeks and is the most common
source of schedule variation. Finalizing your finish, tile, coping, and

## **Step 2: Excavation and Steel (About 1 Week)**

Once permits are approved, visible work begins quickly. The pool layout is
staked precisely to the permitted plans, and an excavator removes soil to the
engineered depth—typically completing the dig in one to three days. From
there, a steel rebar framework is tied across the full shell: walls, floor,
benches, and steps. Crews then install and pressure-test all underground
plumbing lines. A county rough-in inspection is required and must pass before
the next phase begins. This inspection is your protection against plumbing
issues that would otherwise be buried inside a concrete shell.

## **Step 3: Gunite and Shell Curing (1–2 Days + 28-Day Cure)**

Gunite is pneumatically applied concrete sprayed onto the rebar framework to
form the structural shell. For homeowners in [Lake Worth
Beach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lake_Worth_Beach,_Florida) and across the
county, this is often the most exciting day to see in person—it is the moment
the pool takes its permanent shape. After application, the shell requires a
minimum 28-day curing period, during which it must stay moist. Surface cracks
during curing are normal and are addressed when the interior finish is
applied.

## **Step 4: Tile, Coping, Decking, and Equipment (2–5 Weeks)**

With the shell cured, work shifts to the surfaces that define the finished
look. Waterline tile is applied first, followed by the coping—the cap material
at the pool's edge. Travertine, limestone, and porcelain are the most popular
choices in South Florida. The surrounding deck is then laid, and the equipment
pad is set. All mechanical systems—pump, filter, heater, salt system, and
automation—are installed and wired by a licensed electrician. Florida energy
code now requires variable-speed pumps on most new pool installations, which
significantly reduces monthly operating costs.

## **Step 5: Interior Finish, Fill, and Startup (1–2 Weeks)**

The interior finish—white plaster, quartz aggregate, or a pebble finish like
PebbleTec—is the final surface applied to the shell. Once applied, filling
begins immediately. A standard residential pool holds 20,000 to 30,000 gallons
and fills in 24 to 48 hours. A startup technician monitors water chemistry
continuously during the fill to protect the new finish during its critical
curing window. Final county inspections covering building, plumbing, and
electrical are scheduled during this phase. A certificate of completion must
be issued before the pool is officially handed over—do not accept a finished
pool without it. Browse finished pool photos from our recent builds on our

## **Factors That Cause Delays and How to Avoid Them**

  * **Permit Delays:** Incomplete permit submissions restart the review clock. Choose a builder who submits accurate, complete packages the first time.
  * **Change Orders:** Design changes after permit submission require amended plans. Finalize all decisions before the application goes in.
  * **Rainy Season:** South Florida 's wet season (June through September) can slow excavation and outdoor finishing. Projects that begin permitting in late winter typically hit construction before peak rain.
  * **Inspection Queues:** Builders with clean permit records and established relationships with county inspectors move through the queue more efficiently.

## **Frequently Asked Questions**

**How long does pool construction take in West Palm Beach?**

Total timeline from signed contract to first swim is typically 16 to 26 weeks.
Permitting accounts for 4 to 8 weeks; physical construction runs 10 to 16
weeks depending on scope and weather.

**Do I need to be home during construction?**

You do not need to be present daily, but your availability for key
decisions—finish selection, change approvals—affects the schedule. Your
project manager should have a clear process for reaching you when a decision
is needed.

**Can I make changes after construction has started?**

Changes are possible during construction but become progressively more
expensive and disruptive the further along the project is. Changes before
gunite are relatively straightforward. Changes after the shell is
poured—especially to plumbing or pool shape—can require significant rework.
The best way to avoid costly change orders is to finalize every decision,
including finish, tile, coping, and equipment, before the permit is submitted.

**What is the difference between gunite and fiberglass pools?**

Gunite pools are built on-site by spraying concrete onto a steel framework,
allowing full customization of shape, size, depth, and features. Fiberglass
pools are pre-manufactured shells delivered and dropped into an excavated
hole—faster to install but limited to standard manufacturer shapes and sizes.
In South Florida, gunite remains the dominant choice for custom residential
builds because of the design flexibility and long-term durability in the local
climate.
