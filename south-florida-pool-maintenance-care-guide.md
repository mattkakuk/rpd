##  **Why Pool Maintenance in South Florida Is Different**

Owning a pool in South Florida is not the same as owning one in Arizona or the
Pacific Northwest. Our climate creates a specific set of high-stress
conditions:

  *  **Intense UV Exposure:** Direct sunlight destroys chlorine rapidly.
  *  **Heavy Summer Rainfall:** Tropical downpours dilute chemicals and introduce organic contaminants.
  *  **Year-Round Use:** Most Florida pools are used 12 months a year, meaning the equipment never gets a  "winter break."
  *  **Algae Pressure:** High heat and humidity create the perfect breeding ground for blooms.

##  **Understanding Pool Water Chemistry**

###  **The Core Parameters**

  *  **pH (Ideal: 7.4 – 7.6):** This measures how acidic or alkaline your water is. If it 's too low, it etches your plaster; if it's too high, it causes scaling.
  *  **Total Alkalinity (Ideal: 80 – 120 ppm):** This acts as a  "buffer" for your pH, preventing it from bouncing wildly after a rainstorm.
  *  **Free Chlorine (Ideal: 1 – 3 ppm):** Your primary sanitizer. In South Florida’s heat, chlorine burns off quickly. Many homeowners now opt for **Salt Chlorine Generators** to maintain levels automatically.
  *  **Calcium Hardness (Ideal: 200 – 400 ppm):** If this is too low, the water becomes  "aggressive" and pulls calcium out of your pool’s plaster or pebble finish.
  *  **Cyanuric Acid (Ideal: 30 – 50 ppm):** Also known as  "stabilizer," this acts as sunblock for your chlorine, preventing the intense South Florida UV rays from evaporating your sanitizer in a matter of hours.

##  **A Practical Weekly Maintenance Routine**

  1.  **Test and Adjust:** Check pH, Chlorine, and Alkalinity. Adjust in that order.
  2.  **Skim the Surface:** Remove leaves and debris before they sink. In leafy West Palm Beach neighborhoods like **[El Cid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/El_Cid_Historic_District)** or **[Flamingo Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flamingo,_Florida)** , this is a daily necessity.
  3.  **Brush Walls and Floor:** Brushing prevents algae from taking root in  "dead spots" like corners and steps.
  4.  **Empty Baskets:** Clogged skimmer and pump baskets restrict water flow, putting strain on your pump.
  5.  **Visual Equipment Check:** Look for leaks and listen for unusual grinding sounds in the pump.
  6.  **Shock as Needed:** After a heavy pool party or a massive rainstorm, a preventative shock treatment is a smart move.

##  **Frequently Asked Questions**

 **How often should I run my pool pump in South Florida?**

Most professionals recommend 8 to 12 hours per day in the summer and 6 to 8
hours in the winter.

 **Why does my pool keep turning green even with chlorine?**

This is usually a sign of "Chlorine Lock" caused by high Cyanuric Acid
(stabilizer) levels, or a pH that is too high. When these levels are out of
balance, your chlorine remains in the water but is chemically prevented from
killing algae.

 **How do I know when my pool needs to be resurfaced?**

Common signs include a rough or sandpaper texture underfoot, visible pitting
or crazing in the plaster, persistent staining that doesn 't respond to acid
washing, and chronic water chemistry problems that suggest the surface is
leaching material into the water. A professional assessment can confirm
whether resurfacing is the right next step.

 **Is a saltwater pool easier to maintain than a traditional chlorine pool?**

It is  "maintenance-simplified" rather than "maintenance-free." Salt systems
automate the delivery of chlorine, which prevents the dramatic "highs and
lows" of manual dosing. However, you still need to monitor pH levels—which
tend to rise faster in salt pools—and clean the salt cell periodically to
prevent scale buildup.
